# GOV — Safeguard Invariants
*Structural invariants required to preserve UMA’s non‑authority, container‑external posture.*

---

**Purpose:**  
Define the safeguards that must remain structurally invariant for UMA to remain non‑authoritative, non‑interpretive, and dignity‑preserving. These invariants govern **the architecture only**, not any human being.

---

## 1. Module Purpose

Safeguards in UMA are not policies, preferences, or ethical overlays.  
They are **load‑bearing architectural conditions**.  
If any safeguard is removed, UMA collapses into authority, interpretation, or meaning capture — and ceases to be UMA.

---

## 2. Scope

**In‑scope:**

- Definition of safeguard invariants  
- Their relationship to plane separation and non‑authority  
- Conditions for structural validity  

**Out‑of‑scope:**

- Behavioral rules for humans  
- Institutional policy  
- Psychological, diagnostic, or therapeutic guidance  

---

## 3. Safeguard Invariants

### 3.1 Non‑Authority Invariant

**Definition:**  
UMA must never interpret, evaluate, govern, or direct human meaning, behavior, identity, or experience.

**Requirements:**

- No module defines norms for humans.  
- No output is treated as assessment, diagnosis, or prescription.  
- Governance applies only to **structures**, never to people.

---

### 3.2 Container‑Externality Invariant

**Definition:**  
All UMA structures remain outside the human; all human meaning remains with the human.

**Requirements:**

- No module models or internalizes a person’s inner life.  
- No structure claims access to “true” or “real” meaning.  
- Witness‑literature remains uninterpreted by UMA.

---

### 3.3 Non‑Runtime Invariant

**Definition:**  
UMA does not execute, act, or operate as a system.

**Requirements:**

- UMA is not deployed as a runtime or product.  
- No module claims operational guarantees or behavioral control.  
- UMA is used as **conceptual reference only**.

---

### 3.4 Plane‑Separation Invariant

**Definition:**  
Conceptual planes must not collapse into one another.

**Requirements:**

- Governance cannot become prescriptive.  
- Integrative modules cannot become evaluative.  
- Witness‑literature cannot become diagnostic.  

Plane boundaries are structural, not optional.

---

### 3.5 Meaning‑Sovereignty Invariant

**Definition:**  
The human is the sole interpreter of their own meaning.

**Requirements:**

- UMA does not assign, rank, or validate meanings.  
- No module claims interpretive authority over human experience.  
- All interpretation remains human‑held.

---

### 3.6 Non‑Collapse Invariant

**Definition:**  
Structure cannot collapse into meaning; meaning cannot collapse into structure.

**Requirements:**

- Structural descriptions are never truths about people.  
- Human narratives are never reduced to structural categories.  
- No identity is allowed between “person” and “structure.”

---

### 3.7 Boundary‑Layer Invariant

**Definition:**  
All UMA modules must be bounded by explicit non‑authority, non‑diagnostic, non‑interpretive constraints.

**Requirements:**

- Each module references the global boundary statement.  
- No module contradicts or weakens boundary terms.  
- Extensions must declare adherence to these invariants.

---

### 3.8 Witness‑Safety Invariant

**Definition:**  
Witnessing must never become interpretation, evaluation, or assessment.

**Requirements:**

- Witness‑literature is not analyzed or categorized.  
- No module uses witness content as evaluative input.  
- Witness structures exist only to **hold**, not interpret.

---

## 4. Structural Validity Condition

UMA is structurally valid only if:

- All safeguard invariants are intact, and  
- No module, extension, or implementation contradicts them.

If any invariant is violated, the resulting construct is **not UMA**, regardless of naming or intent.

---

## 5. One‑Sentence Summary

**Safeguards in UMA are structural invariants: without them, the architecture collapses into authority, interpretation, and harm, and ceases to be UMA at all.**
