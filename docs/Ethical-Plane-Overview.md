# Ethical Plane Overview
The Ethical Plane defines the stance from which a system is interpreted.  
It is not about rules or structure — it is about posture, humility, and non‑authority.

## 1. Purpose
The Ethical Plane ensures that all interpretation is grounded in:
- humility  
- non‑coercion  
- non‑authority  
- clarity  
- non‑reification  

It answers the question: **“How should we relate to this system?”**

## 2. What Belongs in the Ethical Plane
- ethical stances  
- interpretive commitments  
- humility practices  
- non‑authority framing  
- non‑coercive orientation  
- Sādhana principles  

This plane governs *how* we approach the system, not what the system is.

## 3. What Does NOT Belong Here
- structural diagrams  
- rules or constraints  
- permissions  
- operational governance  
- metaphysical claims  
- psychological authority  

Those belong to other planes.

## 4. Ethical Integrity Rules
To maintain clarity:
- ethics must guide interpretation, not control behavior  
- no ethical note may imply hierarchy or obedience  
- the stance must remain non‑dual and non‑coercive  
- humility overrides certainty  
- no model may be treated as truth  

## 5. Allowed Operations
- framing interpretation  
- clarifying stance  
- grounding analysis in humility  
- preventing authority inflation  
- preventing reification  

## 6. Prohibited Operations
- prescribing behavior  
- enforcing rules  
- creating authority structures  
- psychological influence  
- metaphysical assertions  

## 7. One‑Sentence Summary
The Ethical Plane defines the humble, non‑authoritative stance from which a system is interpreted.
